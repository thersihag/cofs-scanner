Public Administration

Public administration is the implementation of government policy and the management of public affairs, both as an academic discipline and a practical field. It involves planning, organizing, directing, coordinating, and controlling government operations to ensure effective service delivery.

Objectives
  • Implement government policies
  • Deliver public services
  • Ensure accountability and transparency
  • Promote social welfare and development
  • Maintain law and order
Functions
  • Policy Implementation: Executing laws and decisions
  • Resource Management: Managing finances, staff, and materials
  • Public Service Delivery: Providing health, education, sanitation
  • Regulatory Function: Enforcing rules and regulations
  • Developmental Function: Promoting economic and social growth

Principles of Organization

Organizational principles are vital for structuring institutions effectively, guiding task division, coordination, and management to achieve goals efficiently in both public and private sectors, including fisheries governance.

Core Principles
  • Division of Work: Enhances efficiency through specialization
  • Hierarchy: Clear authority chain for discipline
  • Unity of Command: One supervisor per employee
  • Span of Control: Manageable subordinate numbers
  • Delegation: Shares authority for decision-making
  • Coordination: Harmonizes departmental efforts
  • Centralization vs. Decentralization: Balances decision-making
  • Flexibility: Adapts to change and innovation
Significance

Well-applied principles enhance operational efficiency, ensure accountability, and support strategic goals, vital for institutions like fisheries cooperatives.

Regulatory Framework

Role of Central & State Governments in Fisheries and Aquaculture

The Central and State Governments of India play pivotal roles in regulating and promoting sustainable aquaculture and fisheries, ensuring environmental protection, economic growth, and the welfare of fishers through policies, subsidies, and training programs.

Central Government Responsibilities

The Ministry of Fisheries, Animal Husbandry and Dairying oversees fisheries in the Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ), promotes sustainable practices, and supports fishers through various schemes and infrastructure development.

  • Resource Management

    Conducts surveys and assessments of fisheries resources in the EEZ to ensure sustainable exploitation.

  • Financial Support

    Provides subsidies for motorization of traditional craft, fishing gear, and reimbursement of excise duty on High Speed Diesel (HSD) oil.

  • Infrastructure Development

    Develops fishing harbor facilities at major and minor ports and supports renovation or construction of fish farms.

  • Aquaculture Support

    Facilitates establishment of shrimp seed hatcheries with a capacity of 2-5 million.

  • Community Welfare

    Implements the Development of Fishing Villages (DFV) program, providing housing, drinking water, and community halls.

  • Relief Programs

    Offers financial relief to fishers during lean periods to ensure livelihood security.

State Government Responsibilities

State Governments regulate aquaculture operations, enforce environmental standards, and provide training and financial assistance to promote sustainable fisheries practices at the local level.

  • Training Programs

    Offers in-service training for departmental officers and hands-on training for fish farmers through refresher courses and study tours.

  • Merged Schemes

    Integrates Education, Training & Extension, and Utilization of Saline Ground Water schemes to enhance efficiency.

  • Licensing & Regulation

    Oversees licensing and permitting for aquaculture operations to ensure compliance with regulations.

  • Environmental Oversight

    Monitors water quality, sets environmental standards, and establishes harvest limits to promote sustainability.

  • Research & Extension

    Conducts research and provides extension services to support innovation in the fisheries sector.

  • Financial Assistance

    Offers subsidies and financial support to promote sustainable aquaculture and fishing practices.

Established: July 1, 1916

The Zoological Survey of India (ZSI) was founded to focus on animal taxonomy and conservation, providing the scientific foundation for understanding Indiaâ€™s aquatic resources and supporting fisheries development.

Central Marine Fisheries Research Station

Established: February 3, 1947

Initiated on the recommendation of Dr. Baini Prashad, Director of ZSI, the Central Marine Fisheries Research Station (CMFRI) was established in Kochi, Kerala, to advance marine fisheries research and management.

Central Inland Fisheries Research Station

Established: March 17, 1947

Founded in Barrackpore, West Bengal, the Central Inland Fisheries Research Station (CIFRI) was later elevated in 1961 to the Central Institute of Fisheries Technology (CIFT) in Kochi, Kerala, enhancing inland fisheries research.

Land Reforms

Land Reforms Legislation for Aquaculture in India

Land reform legislation in India shapes the aquaculture sector by regulating land use, ensuring equitable access, and promoting sustainable practices, fostering growth while addressing environmental and social challenges.

Introduction to Land Reforms Legislation

Land reform legislation in India was introduced to address inequalities in land ownership and promote social justice. Key objectives included abolishing intermediaries like zamindars, implementing land ceilings, regulating tenancy, and redistributing surplus land to the landless. These reforms aimed to empower small and marginal farmers and boost agricultural productivity. Over time, their scope has expanded to include aquaculture, which relies on land and water resources, ensuring sustainable development and equitable resource use in this growing sector.

Land Ceiling Laws

State-specific laws cap land ownership. Some states classify aquaculture as agriculture, allowing it within ceiling limits, while others impose restrictions, affecting aquaculture expansion.

Land Conversion Norms

Converting agricultural land for aquaculture requires official approval. States like Andhra Pradesh and Tamil Nadu have streamlined procedures to facilitate such conversions.

Tenancy Laws

Legal recognition of tenancy impacts leased land use for aquaculture. Informal tenancy in some states poses risks to aquaculture investments.

Common Property Resources

Community water bodies, managed by Panchayats, can be leased for aquaculture, but challenges remain in ensuring equitable access and effective management.

Coastal Regulation Zone Act

Regulates coastal aquaculture, particularly shrimp farming, with environmental safeguards enforced by the Aquaculture Authority.

Recent Developments

States like Andhra Pradesh have amended land laws to recognize aquaculture as an agricultural activity, offering benefits such as subsidized electricity and simplified land conversion processes. These changes have significantly boosted aquaculture growth, enhanced livelihoods, and promoted sustainable practices in the sector.

Coastal Regulation

Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) in India

The Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) framework protects Indiaâ€™s coastal ecosystems by regulating development activities, ensuring environmental conservation and sustainable livelihoods under the Environment Protection Act, 1986.

What is Coastal Regulation Zone?

The Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) refers to designated coastal areas governed by specific development rules under the Environment Protection Act, 1986. Its primary objective is to conserve the coastal environment, regulate activities along the coast, and safeguard the livelihoods of coastal communities while balancing ecological and economic needs.

CRZ-I: Ecologically Sensitive Areas

Includes mangroves, coral reefs, and other ecologically sensitive zones where no new construction is permitted to protect biodiversity.

CRZ-II: Urban Coastal Areas

Covers developed urban areas near the coast, where regulated construction is allowed following specific guidelines.

CRZ-III: Rural & Undeveloped Areas

Encompasses rural and undeveloped coastal regions with restrictions on large-scale development to preserve natural landscapes.

CRZ-IV: Territorial Waters

Covers water areas up to 12 nautical miles, regulating activities to protect marine ecosystems and resources.
